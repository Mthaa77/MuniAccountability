# MuniAccountability Command™
## Municipal Oversight, Intervention & Recovery Operating System

> **Find the risk. Assign the action. Prove the recovery.**

MuniAccountability Command™ is a source-backed, role-based operating system for South African municipal oversight. It helps provincial oversight teams, municipal recovery teams, committees, researchers, and accountability institutions identify municipal risk early, organise evidence, assign corrective actions, track intervention progress, and prove whether recovery is actually occurring.

It is **not** a generic dashboard and it is **not** a public “name-and-shame” product. Its primary value is operational: it turns fragmented audit reports, financial submissions, recovery plans, and action registers into a practical decision system that users can work from every week.

The product is built around a simple premise:

1. AGSA findings describe what went wrong and where governance, compliance, financial management, procurement, infrastructure, or consequence management failed.
2. National Treasury municipal financial submissions show recurring financial movement, including income, expenditure, cash flow, balance-sheet position, capital spending, and grant drawdowns.
3. Oversight teams need a way to connect those two realities to actions, deadlines, owners, evidence, committee decisions, and recovery outcomes.

MuniAccountability Command bridges that gap.

# 2. Business Opportunity and Product Thesis

## 2.1 The core gap

Municipal accountability data exists, but it is fragmented across annual reports, annexures, spreadsheets, Treasury data cubes, budgets, recovery plans, meeting packs, and manually maintained trackers.

The result is that users repeatedly perform the same work:

- search reports for a municipality;
- compare the current finding with the historical trend;
- inspect whether financial conditions are improving or worsening;
- determine whether an assigned action was completed;
- request updates and supporting evidence;
- assemble an executive or committee briefing;
- revisit the same issue months later with no coherent digital audit trail.

MuniAccountability Command converts that manual cycle into a repeatable system.

## 2.2 Commercial thesis

The most valuable clients are not paying for charts. They are paying for:

- reduced research and briefing time;
- better intervention prioritisation;
- an auditable record of who was assigned to act and when;
- easier progress reporting to senior leadership and committees;
- a reliable shared source of truth across departments;
- less dependence on disconnected spreadsheets and institutional memory;
- faster identification of funding, infrastructure, cash-flow, and recovery risks.

## 2.3 Product moat

The moat is not a colour scheme, map, or AI chatbot. It is the combination of:

1. **Structured municipal accountability data** with page-level source provenance.
2. **A verified action and evidence workflow** that turns findings into managed cases.
3. **A transparent intervention-priority model** that makes risk prioritisation explainable.
4. **Institutional workflow history**, including decisions, comments, deadlines, evidence, escalations, and outcomes.
5. **Source-locked AI assistance** that creates useful briefings without hallucinating.
6. **Public distribution loops** through MuniCheck™ profiles and embeddable municipal widgets.

# 3. Product Vision, Outcomes, and Guardrails

## 3.1 Vision

> Every municipal oversight decision should be traceable to evidence, linked to a clear action, owned by a responsible person, measured over time, and understandable to non-specialists.

## 3.2 Primary business outcomes

| Outcome | Meaning | Evidence of success |
|---|---|---|
| Faster oversight decisions | Users find risk and evidence quickly | Reduced time to prepare an intervention brief |
| Higher corrective-action visibility | Findings cannot disappear into annual reports | % of material issues with tracked action plans |
| Better intervention prioritisation | Teams focus on the municipalities most in need | Weekly queue used in formal review meetings |
| Better executive reporting | Senior leaders get concise, defensible insights | Automatically generated cited briefings used in meetings |
| Recurring enterprise revenue | Client relies on the platform continuously | Annual licence renewal, seat expansion, module adoption |
| Greater public trust | Public layer communicates facts plainly | Growth in verified public profile / widget usage |

## 3.3 Product principles

1. **Evidence before opinion.** Every important fact must link to a source, period, and method.
2. **Action before aesthetics.** The interface must answer “what needs to happen next?” faster than “what looks impressive?”
3. **Explainable by default.** Scores, alerts, recommendations, and AI summaries must reveal their inputs.
4. **Freshness is visible.** Data currency must be obvious beside the number, not hidden in a tooltip.
5. **No unsupported accusations.** The platform surfaces verified findings, risks, and trends; it does not imply criminal guilt or corruption without a source-backed legal basis.
6. **One number, one meaning.** Users should never need to guess what a score, date, period, or status represents.
7. **Role-aware complexity.** Residents get a plain-language view; analysts get depth; executives get decisions and exceptions.
8. **Designed for constrained conditions.** Fast on ordinary mobile connections, readable on projectors, printable for meetings.
9. **Source data remains distinguishable.** AGSA-verified findings, Treasury submissions, client-entered progress updates, and platform-derived calculations must never be mixed without labels.
10. **Human review is a product feature.** High-impact data publication and case closure need accountable review—not blind automation.

## 3.4 Non-goals

The first release will not:

- replace AGSA, National Treasury, a municipal ERP, or statutory audit processes;
- make legal conclusions or allegation-based corruption claims;
- predict municipal collapse using opaque black-box machine learning;
- promise real-time financial information where the source data is periodic;
- become a full municipal finance system or procurement platform;
- ingest every government dataset before proving the core municipal oversight workflow;
- publish unreviewed municipal allegations from public users.

# 6. Core User Experience Strategy

## 6.1 UX objective

The application must feel like a calm, premium, highly capable public-sector command centre—not a dense BI report, a flashy fintech app, or a generic SaaS admin panel.

The interface must help a busy executive answer these questions in **under 30 seconds**:

1. What is urgent?
2. What changed since the last review?
3. Which municipality needs attention?
4. What evidence supports the concern?
5. Who owns the next action?
6. What decision is required from me?

## 6.2 Visual direction

Avoid heavy dark navy and purple “dashboard” styling.

### Recommended visual language

- **Canvas:** warm off-white / light stone background.
- **Surfaces:** white cards with soft, low-elevation shadows.
- **Primary colour:** deep teal / civic green for trusted action and structure.
- **Secondary colour:** South African blue used sparingly for information and links.
- **Risk colours:** accessible amber, coral-red, and green—never relying on colour alone.
- **Typography:** premium humanist sans-serif with strong numerical readability.
- **Corners:** rounded but restrained; enterprise-grade rather than playful.
- **Maps:** light neutral base map with accessible risk overlays.
- **Motion:** subtle state transitions, loading skeletons, change highlights; no distracting animation.
- **Density:** adaptive. Executives see concise summary cards; analysts can expand into tables and evidence.

## 6.3 Information hierarchy

Every major screen follows this hierarchy:

1. **Decision headline** — what needs attention.
2. **Context band** — period, freshness, coverage, filters.
3. **Priority actions** — what the user can do now.
4. **Evidence layer** — why the system says this.
5. **Deep analysis** — tables, time series, source documents.

## 6.4 Universal patterns

### Data Freshness Chip
Every source-derived metric displays a freshness chip:

- `AGSA verified · FY 2024–25`
- `Treasury submission · Q3 2025–26`
- `Pulled 3 days ago`
- `Stale: expected update overdue`
- `Client-entered progress update · awaiting reviewer sign-off`

### Evidence Drawer
Clicking a metric opens an evidence drawer containing:

- value definition;
- source type;
- reporting period;
- formula / calculation where relevant;
- source document link or page reference;
- data owner;
- verification status;
- last refreshed date;
- change history.

### Action Drawer
Clicking “Create action” opens a structured drawer instead of a long form page:

- action title;
- linked finding/risk;
- owner;
- supporting team;
- due date;
- priority;
- required evidence;
- escalation rule;
- current status.

### Command Palette
A global keyboard-friendly command palette lets advanced users jump to:

- a municipality;
- a risk alert;
- an action;
- a briefing;
- an evidence document;
- a saved view;
- “Ask MuniCommand”.

### Saved Views
Users can save contextual views such as:

- “Gauteng high-priority municipalities”;
- “Outstanding MI action evidence”;
- “Recovery plans at risk”;
- “MIG drawdown risk for this quarter”.

3. **Overdue corrective actions**

| Rank | Municipality | Why it is here | Risk level | What changed | Required next action | Owner | Due | Status |

#### E. “Decision required” lane

- this week’s review meetings;

- Every priority item opens a source-backed municipality case file.
- Every critical risk shows a specific reason, not merely a colour.
- User can generate an executive briefing from selected queue items in no more than three actions.

## 8.2 Intervention Queue

### Purpose
Create a defensible, continuously updated worklist of municipalities and issues requiring human attention.

### Queue categories
- `Critical — executive decision required`
- `High — intervention / follow-up required`
- `Medium — analyst review required`
- `Watch — monitor next reporting period`
- `Resolved — retained for history`

### How an item enters the queue
An item can be created by:

1. automatic rule-based risk trigger;
2. AGSA finding ingestion;
3. manual analyst escalation;
4. overdue corrective action;
5. evidence rejection;
6. recovery-plan milestone failure;
7. deteriorating financial indicator;
8. committee request;
9. external source update reviewed by a data steward.

### Queue item structure

```yaml
queue_item:
  id: string
  municipality_id: string
  title: string
  risk_type: audit | financial | grant | infrastructure | recovery | action_overdue | data_quality
  severity: critical | high | medium | watch
  priority_score: number
  reason_summary: string
  trigger_rule_id: string | null
  evidence_refs: [source_reference]
  linked_findings: [finding_id]
  linked_actions: [action_id]
  recommended_next_step: string
  assigned_to: user_id | null
  due_date: date | null
  escalation_status: none | pending | escalated | acknowledged
  created_at: datetime
  updated_at: datetime
```

### UX requirements
- Default sort: priority, then due date, then change recency.
- Explainability panel: “Why is this ranked #2?”
- Bulk actions: assign, tag, add to briefing, request update, defer with reason.
- Filter by province, category, severity, status, owner, data freshness, and source type.
- Distinguish **risk signal** from **confirmed finding** through visible labels.
- User cannot mark a high-risk item resolved without a resolution reason and supporting evidence reference.

## 8.4 Audit Finding → Action Engine

### Purpose
Turn audit findings and material irregularities into accountable, trackable corrective work.

### Core idea
An audit report is not the end of the process. It is the start of a managed remediation workflow.

### Finding types
- Financial statement quality
- Compliance with legislation
- Performance information
- Supply chain management
- Contract management
- Revenue management
- Infrastructure project management
- Asset management
- Consequence management
- Material irregularity
- Financial recovery / fiscal distress
- Service-delivery risk

### Action record requirements

```yaml
action:
  id: string
  title: string
  municipality_id: string
  linked_finding_id: string
  action_type: corrective | investigative | evidence_request | decision | monitoring | escalation
  risk_level: critical | high | medium | low
  outcome_expected: string
  root_cause_addressed: string
  owner: user_id
  supporting_owners: [user_id]
  reviewer: user_id
  due_date: date
  evidence_requirements: [string]
  dependencies: [action_id]
  status: not_started | in_progress | evidence_submitted | under_review | approved | rejected | overdue | closed_with_residual_risk
  completion_rationale: string | null
  source_refs: [source_reference]
  activity_log: [activity]
```

### Workflow

1. Data analyst or system creates finding record.
2. Oversight lead validates classification.
3. Action owner is assigned.
4. Owner receives notification and provides a response plan.
5. Owner uploads evidence or links official documents.
6. Reviewer checks evidence against requirements.
7. Reviewer approves, rejects, requests clarification, or escalates.
8. Closure requires a rationale and residual-risk assessment.
9. Closed actions remain in history and influence future trend analysis.

### Evidence review UX
Evidence review should feel like a high-quality document review experience:

- split screen: action requirements on left, uploaded evidence on right;
- reviewer checklist;
- comments pinned to document pages or file sections;
- approve / reject / request clarification buttons;
- immutable review log;
- visible “evidence still missing” indicator.

### Escalation logic
Examples:

- Critical action overdue by 7 days → escalate to supervisor.
- Action overdue by 30 days → create executive queue item.
- Evidence rejected twice → require oversight review.
- Repeated finding across years → add to repeat-finding radar.

## 8.6 Funding-at-Risk Monitor

### Purpose
Surface financial and infrastructure-delivery conditions that may place public funding, service delivery, or project completion at risk.

### Data inputs
- income and expenditure submissions;
- cash-flow submissions;
- balance-sheet submissions;
- capital spending submissions;
- conditional grant data;
- AGSA infrastructure, procurement, and financial-management findings;
- client-entered recovery-plan status where available.

### Key monitors

| Monitor | What it detects | Typical action |
|---|---|---|
| Grant drawdown risk | Spend / drawdown materially behind expected pace | Request project recovery plan |
| Capital delivery slippage | Capital spend pattern below delivery curve | Review project pipeline and procurement blockers |
| Cash runway pressure | Deteriorating liquidity / insufficient operating cover | Escalate financial recovery review |
| Revenue collection deterioration | Collection weakening relative to billed revenue or peers | Request revenue enhancement intervention |
| Creditor / debt pressure | Financial obligations crowd out service delivery | Review payment plan and funding strategy |
| Salary bill pressure | Personnel cost crowding out core operating spend | Require cost containment review |
| Repeat infrastructure risk | Historic audit findings coincide with current capital underperformance | Prioritise evidence-based project intervention |

### UX requirements
- Show `risk`, `reason`, `period`, `threshold`, and `source` together.
- Never show an “at risk” flag without an explanation.
- Users can adjust peer cohort filters, but the default peer group must be transparent.
- Threshold changes are version-controlled and logged.

## 8.7 Municipal Financial Pulse

### Purpose
Provide a concise, non-accounting-expert-readable view of a municipality’s latest financial direction.

### Essential metrics

| Metric | Definition | User-friendly wording |
|---|---|---|
| Cash runway | Estimated months of operating cover based on current available cash and operating outflow assumptions | “How long can current cash support normal operations?” |
| Revenue collection efficiency | Actual collections relative to billed revenue / comparable metric | “How much of what is billed is actually collected?” |
| Operating budget burn | Spending progression against approved operating budget | “Is operating spending running ahead or behind plan?” |
| Grant drawdown efficiency | Grant utilisation relative to expected period pace | “Is grant-funded delivery moving fast enough?” |
| Capital expenditure progress | Infrastructure / asset spending against planned progression | “Are capital projects moving at the required pace?” |
| Debt exposure | Borrowing and debt servicing burden | “Is debt crowding out money for services?” |
| Personnel pressure | Personnel costs as share of operating expenditure | “How much of the operating budget goes to people costs?” |

### Freshness policy
No financial card may display without:

- source period;
- pulled / refreshed date;
- source label;
- status: current, aging, stale, unavailable, or under review.

## 8.8 Intervention Priority Index

### Purpose
Provide an explainable internal prioritisation score that helps users decide where oversight attention should go first.

### Name and positioning
Use **Intervention Priority Index (IPI)** in institutional contexts.

Do not describe it as a corruption score, performance score, or legal rating. It is an operational prioritisation aid.

### Initial formula components

```text
IPI = Audit Exposure + Repeat-Finding Exposure + Material Irregularity Exposure
    + Financial Deterioration + Funding-at-Risk Exposure + Recovery Execution Risk
    + Governance / Action Overdue Exposure
```

### Score design
- Range: 0–100.
- Higher score = higher recommended oversight attention.
- Each component has a visible contribution.
- Formula version is stored with each score.
- Historical scores remain reproducible after formula updates.
- A score is never displayed without a “why this score” panel.

### Example breakdown

| Component | Score contribution | Explanation |
|---|---:|---|
| Audit exposure | 22/25 | Current outcome and five-year trend indicate persistent control weaknesses |
| Repeat findings | 14/20 | Same root-cause category persisted for three audit cycles |
| Material irregularities | 12/15 | Open MI remains unresolved beyond target date |
| Financial deterioration | 16/20 | Cash and collection trends declined across latest periods |
| Funding risk | 8/10 | Conditional grant drawdown materially behind expected pace |
| Recovery execution | 7/10 | Recovery milestone completion behind plan |

### Governance requirements
- Methodology page is public or client-accessible.
- All thresholds have named owners and approvals.
- User can report a score challenge.
- Score changes cannot overwrite history.
- A municipality’s official response can be recorded and displayed in the internal case file.

- Secret Manager.

- Source periods are mandatory for data records.
- Financial data freshness is mandatory before display.
- All calculated metrics have formula version and inputs retained.
- Changes are versioned; historical records are never silently overwritten.

## 20.3 Non-functional testing

- Load tests for concurrent analyst usage and executive meeting spikes.
- Accessibility testing to WCAG 2.2 AA target.
- Mobile and low-bandwidth testing for MuniCheck.
- Security scan, dependency scan and penetration testing before enterprise rollout.
- Backup restore test.
- Disaster recovery tabletop exercise.
- AI citation validation and hallucination red-team testing.

## 20.4 UX acceptance tests

- An executive can identify top risks within 30 seconds.
- An analyst can locate source evidence for a metric within two clicks.
- A case manager can create, assign and request evidence for an action in under three minutes.
- A researcher can compare four municipalities and generate a source-cited briefing without spreadsheet export.
- A resident can understand a public municipality card without needing accounting knowledge.

# 21. Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Why it matters | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Data licensing uncertainty for Treasury redistribution | May affect commercial API / redistribution model | Treat as Phase 2 legal gate; launch AGSA-first MVP |
| Source data quality / mapping errors | Damages institutional trust | Dual review, provenance, correction workflow, immutable source record |
| Perception of political bias | Could block adoption | Transparent methodology, neutral wording, source-backed design, client governance board |
| Score reputational risk | A score could be misinterpreted as a legal verdict | Position as Intervention Priority Index, show formula and components |
| Scope explosion | Delays pilot and proof | Strict MVP; add modules only after validated demand |
| Sensitive institutional evidence | Creates security and POPIA obligations | Role-based access, tenant isolation, document controls, audit logs |
| AI hallucination | Could undermine core trust proposition | Source-locked RAG, citations mandatory, no-source/no-assertion policy |
| Low adoption after procurement | Software may become shelfware | Build workflows around weekly queue, meetings, actions and briefings |
| Overreliance on a single data source | Limits ongoing value | Phase rollout to Treasury and workflow data after MVP proof |

# Appendix A — Standard Risk and Status Taxonomy

## Risk severity

| Severity | Definition | Default response |
|---|---|---|
| Critical | Material risk needing executive action or immediate escalation | Notify immediately; assign owner; executive review |
| High | Significant risk requiring active intervention or evidence request | Assign within 2 business days |
| Medium | Important concern requiring analyst follow-up | Review in weekly triage |
| Watch | Emerging or stable issue requiring monitoring | Monitor per period |
| Resolved | Action accepted and no active escalation | Retain history and monitor residual risk |

## Action status

| Status | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Not started | Assigned but no work has begun |
| In progress | Owner has started work |
| Evidence submitted | Owner claims completion / progress and submitted support |
| Under review | Reviewer is assessing evidence |
| Approved | Evidence accepted |
| Rejected | Evidence insufficient or action incomplete |
| Overdue | Due date passed without valid completion |
| Escalated | Escalation rule triggered |
| Closed with residual risk | Action complete but risk remains |

# Appendix B — Example Weekly Executive Workflow

1. Executive opens Command Centre Monday morning.
2. Reviews critical changes and overdue actions.
3. Opens Intervention Queue filtered to `Critical` and `High`.
4. Selects three municipalities for the weekly leadership meeting.
5. Opens each Municipality 360° Case File and reviews “What changed?” timeline.
6. Assigns or escalates required actions.
7. Adds selected items to the Weekly Intervention Brief.
8. Uses Ask MuniCommand to draft source-cited oversight questions.
9. Reviews and approves the briefing.
10. Meeting decisions are captured in the Decision Log.
11. Owners receive actions and due dates.
12. The following week, the Command Centre shows what changed and what remains outstanding.

# Appendix C — Definition of Done for Phase 1 Pilot

A Phase 1 pilot is considered complete only when:

- a participating oversight team can log in with assigned roles;
- selected municipalities have verified AGSA-based profiles;
- every headline audit metric includes a source reference;
- users can filter and work an Intervention Queue;
- users can create, assign, evidence, review and close actions;
- users can compare municipalities;
- users can generate a cited executive briefing;
- user activity is audit logged;
- public data is separated from institutional data;
- client stakeholders complete a live use-case test;
- product and data-quality issues are documented and prioritised for the next phase.
